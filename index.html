Introduction

The story begins with an exploration of cinematic history through a dataset provided by Movie Summary Corpus (2013). Movies have always been a mirror of society, reflecting the complexities of human mingling. Through this article, we aim to uncover how trends of movie plots and characters could potentially reflect historical events.

But let's begin from... the beginning right?

This is a historical video from the Roundhay Garden Scene, considered one of the oldest surviving motion pictures.

The oldest film in the database, Roundhay Garden Scene (1888), holds a special place in cinematic history. Produced in the United Kingdom, this silent short film is widely recognized as the world's earliest surviving motion picture. With a runtime of just 3 seconds, it provides a glimpse into the very beginnings of filmmaking. The film, shot in black-and-white, is categorized as a silent, indie short film. While it lacks a plot description, its significance lies in its pioneering use of moving images to capture everyday life. Directed by Louis Le Prince, it features a brief scene of people walking in a garden—a simple yet groundbreaking moment that marked the dawn of a new art form.

The Initial Vision: A Tale of Two Rivals

Originally, our analysis aimed to compare American movies with those from Russia, driven by the long-standing rivalry between the two nations. This rivalry, steeped in decades of proxy wars, economic and political differences, and iconic historical moments—such as the Cold War, the Space Race, and the fall of the Berlin Wall—offered a unique opportunity to explore contrasting cinematic perspectives.

The timeline of significant events in the U.S.-Russia relationship aligns closely with the period covered by the dataset. This seemed to provide a fertile ground for comparative analysis. However, this ambition was met with a challenge: dataset bias.

Why Focus Solely on American Movies?

While comparing the two nations’ cinemas was an exciting proposition, the dataset proved, not surprisingly at this point, that U.S. cinema dominates in terms of quantity, diversity, and completeness of data, making any meaningful comparison with Russian films difficult. This lack of balance between the two sides of the rivalry hindered our ability to draw fair conclusions.

As a result, we decided to shift our focus to American cinema (with some intersting exceptions). However, we will still examine the bipolar perspective of the Cold War through the lens of U.S. cinema, which often reflects the ideological struggles of the time. For example, the Vietnam War.

Collaboration in Movies Between the USA and other Countries

An interesting dimension of our analysis lies in exploring international collaborations in filmmaking. To visualize this, we created a heat map that displays the normalized fraction of movies co-produced by the USA with other countries over time, using time bins of 10 years. This heat map provides an effective lens to examine historical and geopolitical links to cinematic partnerships.

Key Observations:

  • Collaborations with Allies: The USA has consistently collaborated with countries such as France and the United Kingdom over the decades. These collaborations reflect long-standing cultural and political alliances, especially in the aftermath of WWII and during the Cold War era.
  • Rivals Avoided: Unsurprisingly, the USA has had no significant cinematic collaborations with its key geopolitical rivals, Russia and China. This aligns with historical tensions stemming from the Cold War, ideological differences, and trade barriers.
  • Japan: A Unique Case: Japan stands out as a country where collaboration trends evolve over time. After WWII, Japan gradually opened up to the USA, leading to an increase in cinematic partnerships. This mirrors Japan’s broader post-war economic and cultural integration with Western powers.
  • Global Influence: The USA's dominance in cinema is reflected in its ability to engage in numerous partnerships worldwide, albeit selectively. This pattern underscores how soft power operates in shaping international relationships, with cinema serving as a cultural bridge—or boundary—depending on the geopolitical landscape.

The heat map thus offers a visual narrative of how cinema intertwines with diplomacy and global relations, spotlighting patterns of collaboration and exclusion shaped by historical context.

Middle East (Post-2004)

The early 21st century saw an increase in U.S. films focusing on the Middle East, especially in the aftermath of the Iraq War in 2003. These films often reflect Hollywood's reaction to the geopolitical and military dynamics, delving into themes such as terrorism, the ethics of war, and cultural clashes. Some notable examples... The almost absence of Middle East-themed films in earlier decades highlights that Hollywood's narrative choices could be influenced by contemporaneous political events and media coverage.

Nazi (1940s and 1960s-70s)

During the 1940s, Nazi-related films were primarily produced as wartime propaganda to rally support against Axis powers during World War II. These films portrayed the Nazis as clear antagonists, often emphasizing themes of heroism, sacrifice, and the fight for freedom.

In the 1960s and 70s, the resurgence of Nazi-related themes likely stemmed from Cold War anxieties. Filmmakers revisited authoritarian and totalitarian regimes, drawing parallels between Nazi ideologies and the oppressive nature of other adversaries. This period also witnessed a shift in tone, with more nuanced storytelling exploring the psychological and moral complexities of war.

Vietnam (1960s-80s)

The Vietnam War left a profound imprint on American cinema, producing two distinct waves of films.

  • 1960s-70s: Early films depicted the Vietnam War through a lens of nationalism and heroism, reflecting the initial public sentiment and government stance.
  • 1980s: A critical reappraisal emerged as filmmakers began to question the morality and consequences of the war. This era produced iconic anti-war films that examined the psychological toll on soldiers, the impact on Vietnamese civilians, and the divisive effect on American society. These films often featured raw and introspective narratives, challenging audiences to grapple with the broader implications of war.

Connecting the Themes

These three thematic arcs underscore how Hollywood adapts its narratives to align with the socio-political climate of the time. By analyzing keywords and themes in movie summaries, we can trace a cultural dialogue that mirrors evolving societal concerns—from the fear of totalitarian regimes to the aftermath of war and the complexities of geopolitical conflicts.

This analysis underscores how each nation's film industry reflects its unique historical experiences, using cinema to interpret and narrate the complexities of war.

Part 2: Social Changes

This section explores the portrayal of social change in cinema, focusing on the evolution of societal norms and struggles, such as civil rights, feminism, and movements for equality. We analyzed movies through two main approaches:

  1. Genre Grouping by Semantic Themes: Movies were grouped into genres strongly associated with social themes, such as drama, biography, and historical fiction. These genres often center on narratives that address societal issues, personal struggles, and transformations.
  2. Keyword Analysis of Synopses: Keywords related to social movements—such as "activism," "feminism," "civil rights," "equality," and "protest"—were identified in movie summaries. These terms were used to track trends in cinematic storytelling over time, highlighting the industry's response to key historical events.

Observations and Insights:

  • Civil Rights Movement (1950s–1970s): A surge in movies addressing racial inequality and justice was observed during this period. Films tackled themes of segregation, discrimination, and empowerment, reflecting the fight for civil rights in America.
  • Feminism and Gender Equality: Waves of feminist movements were mirrored in cinema, with films showcasing strong female protagonists, challenges to patriarchal norms, and narratives advocating for gender equality. Peaks in these themes aligned with the second wave of feminism in the 1960s–70s and later during the resurgence of feminist discourse in the 1990s.
  • Post-Colonial and Cultural Identity: Movies in the 1970s and 1980s often explored themes of identity, heritage, and the aftermath of colonialism, particularly in nations undergoing significant social transformations.
  • The LGBTQ+ Movement: Representation of LGBTQ+ issues gained prominence starting in the 1990s, with a growing number of films focusing on acceptance, rights, and the challenges faced by these communities.
  • Globalization and Multiculturalism (2000s): As globalization became a defining feature of modern societies, cinema began to explore narratives of migration, cultural integration, and diversity, emphasizing shared human experiences.

Through this analysis, we aim to connect these cinematic trends with the broader social transformations of their times, showing how films serve as cultural artifacts that document and influence societal change.
